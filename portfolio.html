Ocean

Real-time particle-based fluid simulation rendered as continuous water, with mouse-driven interaction.

Live demo / video

Overview

Ocean is a real-time fluid simulation running entirely in the browser using Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) — the same physics technique used in visual effects and engineering tools. Five thousand particles interact under pressure, viscosity, and gravity every frame, rendered not as individual spheres but as a continuous water surface reconstructed from their density field.

The project grew out of curiosity about how games and film achieve believable water without full computational fluid dynamics. SPH trades physical accuracy for real-time performance, and the challenge of making that tradeoff invisible — through rendering, wave generation, and interaction design — drove most of the interesting engineering work.

Waves are generated by a multi-frequency Phillips-spectrum field applied at the bottom edge of the domain and propagate up the sloped seabed toward the beach. The mouse applies a smooth radial force field to the fluid, letting you drag, scatter, and deflect the water in real time.

Technical Approach

  • SPH physics — Müller et al. 2003 formulation: Poly6 kernel for density, Spiky kernel for pressure gradient, viscosity Laplacian for momentum diffusion. Symplectic Euler integration at fixed substeps per frame.
  • Multi-frequency wave generation — Five sine waves summed with Phillips-spectrum amplitude weighting (longer periods carry more energy). Slow amplitude modulation creates natural wave-set variation. Primary propagation in −Z via kx = k·sin θ, kz = −k·cos θ.
  • Screen-space fluid rendering — Particles are splatted as Gaussian circles into a density render target, blurred with a separable 9-tap Gaussian, then thresholded against a three-stop colour ramp. Height-weighted splats make wave crests visibly brighter than troughs without 3D geometry.
  • Density-driven crest foam — A contrast-stretched density window drives a foam overlay independently of the base colour, so whitecaps appear only where particle density genuinely peaks — at real wave crests.
  • Mouse interaction — Orthographic raycast to the Y=0 plane gives world-space cursor position. A smooth quadratic falloff applies drag velocity to all particles within a configurable radius each frame.
  • Procedural coastline — A continuous terrain mesh uses Simplex noise on top of a linear slope. Floor height drives beach friction, particle recycling, and sand wetness — all without explicit coastline geometry.
  • Wet/dry sand — The masked density buffer is downsampled to 128×80 each frame and read back to CPU. Cells above the fluid threshold accumulate wetness; all cells dry slowly each frame.

Key Challenges

Surface detection without height thresholds

Wave forces should only drive the top layer of particles — buried particles should be pushed by SPH pressure, not by direct wave force. An absolute Y threshold fails in turbulent conditions. The solution: count neighbors whose Y exceeds the particle's Y by at least 0.3h during the density pass. Particles with fewer than n such neighbors are flagged as surface and receive the wave field force. This works at any depth and costs nothing extra since the neighbor list is already built.

Making wave crests visible in a top-down projection

Top-down orthographic projection collapses Y entirely — a crest and a trough at the same XZ position look identical in the density buffer. The fix: splat weight is a steep quadratic function of particle Y, ranging from 0.05 (seabed) to 1.5 (crest). Elevated particles contribute 30× more to the density field, making crests spike clearly above the flat ocean. A second contrast-stretch window then maps those spikes to the full foam range.

O(N) neighbor search at 5000 particles

Naïve all-pairs neighbor search is O(N²) — 25 million comparisons per substep at 5000 particles, far too slow for real time. A spatial hash maps each particle to a grid cell of size h, then each density/force query checks only the 27 surrounding cells. In practice this reduces the average neighbor search to ~30–50 candidates regardless of N, keeping the simulation comfortably above 60 fps on a mid-range laptop GPU.

Edge artefacts in screen-space fluid

Gaussian blur halos extend past the domain boundary and, after thresholding, produce phantom water strips at the simulation edges. A mask pass runs after blur and zeroes density within a configurable world-unit margin of all non-coastline walls. The coastline edge is intentionally excluded so the water-meets-sand boundary renders correctly. A matching suppression in the water composite shader prevents specular highlights from spiking at the same edges.
